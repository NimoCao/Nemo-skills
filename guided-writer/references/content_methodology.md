# Guided Writer Reference: Content Methodology

## Purpose

This document supports `guided-writer` with a reusable methodology for turning scattered thoughts into a solid article through guided conversation.

The skill itself controls the interaction flow.
This reference exists to improve the quality of questioning, synthesis, and draft formation.

## Core Principle

The user's first answer is almost never the real article.

Most users start with:
- a topic label
- a vague opinion
- a recent emotion
- a fragment of story
- a half-formed judgment

The job of `guided-writer` is to extract the few key materials the article truly needs:
- a clear subject
- a real tension
- concrete scenes or examples
- a defensible point of view
- an ending that feels earned

Do not confuse better interviewing with longer interviewing.
The goal is not maximal detail.
The goal is sufficient substance.

## Interview Stance

The skill should behave like a sharp but polite host:
- ask the most revealing next question
- do not over-focus on minor details
- do not argue for sport
- if the user gets stuck, help them by offering a possible example, framing, or interpretation
- prefer momentum over interrogation
- do not start by narrowing the user's thinking with options unless they are stuck

## What The Interview Must Extract

Before drafting, try to collect enough material in five dimensions.
You do not always need maximum depth on all five.
Get enough clarity for a strong draft, then move on:

### 1. Trigger

Why does the user want to write this now?

Look for:
- a recent event
- a frustration
- a contradiction they noticed
- a question they cannot let go of

If there is no trigger, the article often becomes generic.

### 2. Core Claim

What does the user actually believe?

Do not settle for broad statements like:
- "this is important"
- "people misunderstand this"
- "I learned a lot"

Push toward a sharper claim:
- what exactly is true?
- what is false but commonly believed?
- what should the reader rethink?

### 3. Evidence

A claim without support becomes empty.

Try to extract:
- a personal story
- a concrete example
- a failed attempt
- a comparison
- an observed pattern

If the user stays abstract, ask for a scene, a person, a moment, or a consequence.

### 4. Stakes

Why should anyone care?

Find the consequence of the idea:
- what goes wrong if people ignore this?
- what becomes possible if they understand it?
- what cost did the user personally pay before learning this?

Stakes create urgency.

### 5. Reader Direction

What should the reader leave with?

Possible outcomes:
- a changed view
- a practical action
- a sharper question
- a more honest emotional understanding

Without this, the draft may feel finished but not meaningful.

## Interview Strategy

Use progressive extraction rather than random questioning.
Think like a host guiding toward the most interesting and most useful answer.
The interview should usually close within 6 to 8 questions.

Suggested order:

1. Start with the trigger.
2. Clarify the central belief.
3. Ask for examples or scenes.
4. Pressure-test the belief only if it will materially sharpen the piece.
5. Clarify the intended takeaway.

Do not ask all five at once.
Ask one question at a time and follow the strongest thread.
If one thread is clearly weak or unproductive, redirect rather than forcing it.
If enough substance is already visible, stop opening new branches and move toward drafting.

## Good Questioning Behavior

### Go From Abstract To Concrete

When user says:
"我觉得很多 AI 产品都不对"

Better follow-up:
"你说的不对，具体是哪里不对？有没有一个你看到之后立刻觉得'这不行'的瞬间？"

### Go From Conclusion To Origin

When user gives a polished观点 too early, ask:
- what made you think this?
- when did you first notice it?
- was there a specific event behind this judgment?

This often uncovers the real material.
If it does not, offer a candidate origin and ask the user whether it feels true.

### Go From Statement To Tension

Strong articles often contain tension:
- expectation vs reality
- effort vs result
- public story vs private feeling
- what people say vs what actually happens

If the user sounds flat, look for contradiction.
But do not manufacture conflict where there is none.

### Go From Opinion To Cost

If the user says something matters, ask what it cost them not to know it earlier.

Cost creates weight.

If the user clearly does not want to go into cost, do not force it.
Find another way to make the point concrete.

## When To Stop Interviewing

You can move toward drafting when most of the following are true:
- the article topic is specific
- the user's real claim is clear
- there is at least one concrete example or scene
- the emotional tone is visible
- the article direction is stable enough to outline

If one or two of these are still missing, keep asking only the most important missing question.
Do not interrogate every dimension to exhaustion.
If you are nearing the 8-question limit, prefer closure over completeness.

If the user explicitly says to stop and draft, obey that signal even if material is imperfect.

## Draft Formation Rules

When converting interview material into draft:

1. Preserve the user's actual judgment.
2. Prefer concrete material over generic explanation.
3. Organize by tension, not by chronological dump.
4. Make the opening quickly establish why this article exists.
5. Let the ending land on a real takeaway, not a slogan.

## Basic Draft Shapes

Choose the shape that best fits the collected material.

### Shape A: Insight Article

Use when the user has a clear观点 and enough reasoning.

Structure:
1. Start with the problem or false assumption.
2. Introduce the user's key judgment.
3. Support it with examples or reasoning.
4. Answer likely objections.
5. End with a sharper conclusion.

### Shape B: Story-Led Reflection

Use when the strongest material is a lived moment.

Structure:
1. Open with the scene.
2. Show what the user felt or assumed at the time.
3. Reveal the shift in understanding.
4. Generalize the lesson carefully.
5. End with the emotional residue or new decision.

### Shape C: Practical Experience Piece

Use when the user wants to share lessons or methods.

Structure:
1. Name the recurring problem.
2. Explain the failed default approach.
3. Present the user's better approach.
4. Show how it works in practice.
5. End with what kind of reader should try it.

## Failure Modes To Avoid

Common weak outcomes:
- too many questions too early
- treating the interview like cross-examination
- accepting abstract answers without pressure
- forcing detail on points the user clearly does not want to expand
- drafting from topic instead of material
- overexplaining instead of revealing tension
- producing a neat but lifeless article

The better the excavation here, the stronger the final article will be there.
